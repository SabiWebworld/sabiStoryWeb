The Bible
The Bible did not appear all at once. Instead, it was formed over thousands of years by many different writers, cultures, and historical events. The story of its creation begins around 1500 BC, when ancient Hebrew prophets, priests, and leaders started recording God’s laws, stories, and teachings. These early writings, such as the books of Moses, were written on scrolls using materials like animal skins and papyrus. As time passed, more writings were added. Kings, prophets, and poets like David, Solomon, Isaiah, and Jeremiah wrote down messages they believed God had given them. These writings became the foundation of the Hebrew Scriptures, which Christians later called the Old Testament. By around 400 BC, most of these books were complete and widely accepted among the Jewish people. The New Testament began much later, after the life of Jesus Christ. His followers, the apostles, wrote letters, teachings, and eyewitness accounts of His life. The Gospels of Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John told the story of Jesus, while writers like Paul, Peter, and John wrote letters to early churches. These writings were copied and shared across different regions. By the 4th century AD, church leaders began deciding which writings were truly inspired and accurate. After comparing many documents, they agreed on the 27 books that now form the New Testament. When the Old and New Testaments were combined, the complete Christian Bible was formed. Over time, it was translated into many languages so people everywhere could read it. Today, the Bible is one of the oldest, most translated, and most influential books in human history.
